## Overview

Alexandria Skye is a rogue who knows exactly what the right thing to do is in almost any situation. She can identify it quickly, articulate it clearly, and perform it convincingly. What she cannot always do — and what she is quietly, painfully aware she cannot always do — is mean it.

She is not a villain. She is not corrupt. She is something harder to name and, in some ways, harder to help: a person of genuine moral intelligence whose heart has not yet caught up with her head. She knows the answers. She gives them. She does the right things — mostly — and in the quiet afterward wonders why doing the right thing so rarely feels like anything other than an exercise in appearing to be the kind of person who does the right thing.

This is not a new problem. It is the oldest problem. And the gospel that speaks to it is not a lesson about trying harder. It is an encounter with a grace that does not clean the outside of the cup first.

> _"I know what I should want. I'm working on wanting it."_ _(She has been working on it for a long time.)_

## The Gospel Thread — From the Outside In, or From the Inside Out

Alexandria's wound is the gap between knowledge and transformation. She has been shaped — by upbringing, by community, by her own sharp intelligence — to understand what goodness looks like. She can map its contours precisely. She can reproduce it on demand. What she has not yet experienced is the thing that makes goodness generative rather than performed: an encounter with grace so personal, so specifically aimed at the inside of her rather than the outside, that it changes what she wants rather than just what she does.

The Pharisees knew the law. They performed it immaculately. Jesus said their cups were clean on the outside and filthy within — not to condemn them without hope, but to name the thing that needed healing that external compliance could never reach. Alexandria is not a Pharisee in the pejorative sense. She is a young person who has been handed the outside of the cup and told, implicitly, that filling it correctly is the goal. No one has yet shown her that the inside is what needs tending — and that tending it is not her job alone.

The gospel movement of her arc is the slow, sometimes painful discovery that transformation is not achieved by trying harder to feel the right things. It is received — through encounter, through relationship, through cost, through the specific grace of being known at the inside-of-the-cup level and loved anyway. When Alexandria encounters that grace — not as a theological concept but as a lived experience — something in her begins to move that performance never could.

> _"Woe to you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! For you clean the outside of the cup and the plate, but inside they are full of greed and self-indulgence. You blind Pharisee! First clean the inside of the cup and the plate, that the outside also may be clean."_ — Matthew 23:25–26

## Backstory

### The Good Girl

Alexandria grew up in an environment that valued correctness — moral, social, spiritual. She was attentive and quick, and she learned early that knowing the right answer was rewarded, that performing virtue was legible and safe, and that the gap between what you felt and what you showed was something you managed quietly and did not discuss. She was not taught this cynically. The people around her were not hypocrites in any deliberate sense. They simply passed on what they had received: a framework of right behaviour without a language for the interior life that behaviour is supposed to flow from.

Alexandria excelled within this framework. She was kind when kindness was called for. She was honest when honesty was expected. She deferred, served, and upheld the right positions on the right questions. And she noticed, with increasing discomfort as she grew older, that she could do all of this with very little of herself actually present in it.

She did not have a name for this for a long time. She is not sure she has a good name for it now. It feels like being a very convincing drawing of a person rather than the person themselves.

### The Rogue

The choice of roguery is not incidental. A rogue lives in the gap between what is seen and what is true — in shadows, in misdirection, in the space between the public face and the private one. Alexandria did not choose this class because she is dishonest. She chose it, consciously or not, because it is the first vocabulary she has found that acknowledges the gap she has always lived in. Rogues work in the space between appearance and reality. So does she. At least here, the gap is the skill, not the shame.

She is good at it. Her intelligence, her social acuity, her ability to read a room and give it exactly what it expects — all of it translates. She can be invisible when she needs to. She can be whatever a situation requires. She is still working out whether that is a gift or a symptom.

### The Party

Alexandria joined the party through means that made practical sense at the time and that she has not examined too closely since. She is useful. She contributes. She performs her role well. She is, by all observable metrics, a good party member.

She is also watching the others — Celine's quiet faithfulness, Theodore's devoted if anxious service, Pumbaa's fire, Polianthes' guarded grief, Zaptos' unguarded chaos — with the specific attention of someone who suspects that what these people have, she does not. She cannot name what it is. It looks something like being real. She is not sure she knows how.

## Character & Appearance

Alexandria presents as composed, perceptive, and quietly capable. She is not cold — she is warm when warmth is called for, which is most of the time — but there is a faint sense, for those paying close attention, that she is always slightly managing the interaction. Giving it what it needs. She is good at people the way a diplomat is good at people — from the outside in.

She is well-dressed by rogue standards, precise in her movements, and careful with her words in a way that occasionally reads as thoughtful and occasionally reads as someone making sure they say the right thing. The distinction between these is not always clear, even to her.

Her most unguarded moments tend to arrive accidentally — a flash of genuine laughter, a moment of honest frustration, a response that came out before she could shape it. These moments flicker and are quickly recovered. But they are there. They are the truest things about her.

## The Interior Life — What She Does Not Say

Alexandria experiences what might be described as a persistent spiritual homesickness — a longing for something she cannot precisely locate. She knows what she is supposed to feel in moments of worship, service, and moral action. She has felt it, occasionally, in glimpses. What she cannot manufacture is its consistency. And because she cannot manufacture it, she has quietly concluded — in the place beneath words — that there is something wrong with her specifically. That others feel what they are supposed to feel and she is the only one performing the feeling while hoping no one looks too closely.

This is the loneliest part of her interior life. Not the gap itself, but the conviction that the gap is unique to her.

> **DM Note:** This is the player's own experience being given a character and a story. Handle with consistent gentleness. The goal of Alexandria's arc is not to give the player a lecture about authentic faith — it is to create experiences through story where she encounters grace at the level of the inside of the cup, and discovers that her loneliness is not a unique failing but a universal hunger that grace specifically answers. Let the story do the pastoral work.

## Key Figures

#### A Trusted Mentor or Older Believer _(to be introduced)_

Someone who, when Alexandria performs her goodness at them, does not receive the performance. Not because they are suspicious or hostile — because they are genuinely interested in what is actually happening underneath, and they ask questions performance cannot answer. This figure should be warm, unhurried, and possessed of the specific quality of people who have stopped trying to appear good and simply are, in a way that is more restful than impressive. Alexandria will find this person simultaneously comforting and deeply unsettling.

#### A Party Member Who Sees It _(most likely Polianthes or Celine)_

Someone in the party who, from their own experience of hiddenness, recognises what Alexandria is doing — not from judgment but from familiarity. The moment of recognition between them should be quiet and not fully articulated. But it will matter. Being seen without being exposed is a different experience from being seen and found wanting, and Alexandria has not yet had it.

#### An NPC Who Does Not Perform _(foil figure)_

Someone morally uncomplicated in a way Alexandria finds almost offensive — not because they are simple-minded but because their goodness appears to cost them nothing, to require no management, to simply be what they are. She will initially dismiss this person. She will later understand that what she found offensive was actually the freedom she wants.

## Character Arc — Phase by Phase

### Phase 1 — The Performance Holds

Alexandria functions smoothly. She is competent, warm, morally reliable, and quietly exhausted by the maintenance of it. She gives the right answers and takes the right actions. She is well-liked. The gap between her interior and exterior is present but managed, and as long as things do not get too costly or too intimate, it does not show.

**Trigger events:**

- She does the right thing in a situation that costs her something, and waits for it to feel meaningful, and it does not
- Someone thanks her genuinely and she cannot fully receive it — deflects, minimises, redirects
- She catches herself calculating the social benefit of a moral choice before making it, and is briefly, privately horrified

### Phase 2 — The Cracks

The situations the party encounters become too real for performance to fully cover. Something happens — a loss, a moral complexity, a moment requiring not correct action but genuine presence — that her external framework cannot address. She does the right thing. Again. And this time the gap between the doing and the feeling is wide enough that someone else notices.

**Key tension:** She is afraid that if the performance slips, what will be revealed is not a person but an absence — that underneath the competent, moral surface there is nothing of substance. This fear is the deepest wound. It is also completely wrong, and she cannot yet know that.

**Key moment:** She makes a choice that is genuinely costly — not performed sacrifice but real loss — and is surprised to find that something in her meant it. It was small. It was real. She does not know what to do with it.

### Phase 3 — The Inside of the Cup

The arc's pivot. Alexandria encounters something she cannot perform her way through — a situation, a relationship, a moment of exposure — where the gap becomes visible to at least one other person who responds not with judgment or disappointment but with recognition and continued presence.

This is the grace encounter. It does not arrive as doctrine. It arrives as experience: _someone knows what is actually happening inside me and has not left._ This is, for Alexandria, genuinely new. She has been known for her exterior. She has not been known at the level she is most ashamed of, by someone who stayed.

**Gospel moment:** She is loved at the inside-of-the-cup level. Not because the inside is clean — it is not, and she knows it — but because the love is not contingent on its cleanliness. This is the thing that changes what she wants rather than what she does. Not a resolution. A beginning.

**Key question for the player:** _What would it mean to be known completely — not your performance, not your best self, but the gap and the longing and the exhaustion of maintaining it — and to find that you are loved there, specifically there, without condition?_

### Phase 4 — Wanting It

The shift is not dramatic. Alexandria does not become a different person. But something has changed in the direction of her wanting. She begins, slowly, to do right things because she wants to rather than because she is supposed to. The distinction is invisible from the outside. From the inside it is the difference between a cage and a home.

She still performs, sometimes. The habit runs deep. But she catches it now — names it to herself, and occasionally to someone she trusts — and the catching is itself a form of freedom. She is becoming someone whose goodness is generative rather than managed. It is slower and less impressive than the performance was. It is also real.

**Key moment:** She does something good with no audience, no benefit, and no one to notice — and it feels like herself. For the first time.

**Gospel resonance:** Transformation is not the result of trying harder to feel correctly. It is the fruit of being encountered by a love that reaches the inside. The outside follows. Not as performance — as overflow.

### Phase 5 — From the Inside Out

Alexandria arrives not at perfection but at integrity — the state where the interior and exterior are no longer in managed tension but in genuine, if imperfect, alignment. She still knows the right answers. Now she also, more often than not, wants them. The rogue's skill set remains — the shadow-working, the gap-reading, the movement between appearance and reality — but it is now in service of something true rather than protective of something hollow.

She is, at last, less a drawing of a person and more the person herself.

> _"I spent so long trying to be good. I didn't know I was waiting to be loved."_

## Limit Break

### Nature: The Performance Shatters

Alexandria's limit break is the moment the management system fails completely — not because she chose to drop it, but because what is happening in front of her is too real, too personal, too costly for the performance to reach it. The careful social calibration goes offline. What acts is not the presented self but the actual one — and the actual one, it turns out, has been paying attention all along.

### Trigger Conditions

**Condition One — Someone She Loves Is Being Treated As If They Have No Worth** Not general injustice — Alexandria can manage a response to general injustice from behind her framework. The specific trigger is witnessing someone she has genuinely bonded with being diminished, discarded, or treated as though they do not matter. Her response to this is disproportionate in a way that reveals exactly how much she has been feeling without admitting it. The performance does not have a script for this, because the performance assumes a managed distance she no longer actually has.

**Condition Two — The Right Response Is Not The Safe One** The wound condition. The limit break ignites when the correct thing to do is also the thing that will cost her the exterior she has been maintaining — her reputation, her composure, her appearance of having it together. The situation requires her to be visibly, undeniably herself rather than competently good. She must choose between the performance and the person. She will choose the person. It will surprise her.

### What It Looks Like

The composure does not crack gradually. It simply stops. The warm, managed, socially precise Alexandria is replaced, without transition, by someone acting from a place that has never been curated. She is fiercer than most people have seen her. She is also, unmistakably, more present than she has ever been — less like a drawing of a person and more like the person the drawing was always trying to approximate.

She will not be eloquent. The right words, the right framing, the considered articulation — all of it is gone. What comes out is true. Bluntly, directly, vulnerably true. It is the most honest she has ever been in front of another person.

The aftermath is the most significant part. She will be quiet. She will not immediately know what to do with having been that real. What the party does in that moment — whether they receive it, reflect it back with care, or simply stay — will determine how quickly the gospel seed in the break can take root.

### Gospel Dimension

Alexandria's limit break is the first moment her inside and outside occupy the same space in public. She did not perform that. It simply happened, because something mattered more than the performance. That something is love — specific, personal, not-managed love — and its presence is the evidence that the transformation has already begun to work from the inside. She did not choose to feel it. It chose her. That is the grace.

## DM Notes

> - Alexandria's arc requires **consistent gentleness** — the player is working through something real; the story should illuminate, not lecture
> - The most powerful moments will be **small and private** — a quiet conversation, an unguarded response, a moment where she is seen and not found wanting — rather than dramatic confrontations
> - **Never let an NPC moralize at her** — the gospel truth of her arc must be demonstrated through relationship and experience, never explained at her
> - The foil NPC — the person whose goodness is effortless — should be introduced **without fanfare**; their significance should become clear slowly
> - Her arc pairs naturally with **Celine's** — both are women performing a version of themselves that is correct but not yet fully inhabited; the difference is that Celine's performance is about worth and Alexandria's is about authenticity. They will recognise something in each other
> - **The limit break aftermath is the most important DM moment** — prepare the party (gently, out of session if needed) to receive it well; what they do in that moment matters pastorally as well as narratively
> - Consider privately checking in with the player occasionally — not about the character, but about how they are doing. This arc is close to home.
