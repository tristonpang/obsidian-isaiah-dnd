## Overview

Theodore is a paladin who became one not because he sought power or heard a thundering divine command, but because he was exhausted and afraid and a presence steadied him anyway. He is a healer first — precise, disciplined, deeply attentive to the people around him — and a fighter second, reluctantly. He sees adventuring not as glory-seeking but as an extension of a healer's duty: going where institutions cannot reach, staying with people who have been left behind.

He is also a man quietly crushing himself under a weight he was never meant to carry.

Theodore believes, in the place beneath words, that outcomes are his responsibility. That if he had been better — faster, smarter, more prepared — the people who died on his watch would still be alive. He does not say this aloud. He is not dramatic about it. He simply works harder, prepares more carefully, holds himself to a standard no human being could meet, and carries every failure like a stone added to a pile he has been building since childhood.

The gospel question at the heart of his arc is this: _can you trust a grace that does not require you to be perfect first?_

---

## The Gospel Thread — Faithfulness Over Perfection

Theodore's wound is a theology of earning. He came to faith through discipline and responsibility, and somewhere along the way the two became fused in a way that has quietly warped both. He serves faithfully — genuinely, sacrificially — but beneath the service is a transaction he is running in his own mind: _if I do enough, if I get it right, no one else has to suffer for my failures._

This is not faith. It is control dressed in the language of devotion.

The movement of his arc is the slow, costly dismantling of that transaction — not the dismantling of his discipline or his care, but the untangling of his worth from his performance. He must learn, at a level deeper than intellectual agreement, that he was called not because he was sufficient but because he was loved. That faithfulness is not the same as perfection. That staying with someone through loss is not failure — it is the very thing he was made for.

> _"You are not the one who saves. You are the one who serves."_

This is the truth he received at his calling. He believes it in his head. He has not yet believed it in his bones.

## Backstory

### The Village & The Promise

Theodore grew up in a small village struck by a slow, creeping illness — something between plague and curse that no travelling healer who passed through could fully arrest. As a boy he helped however he could: carrying water, cleaning wounds, sitting with the dying when sitting was all that was left to do. He watched the healers work — careful, methodical, serious — and made a quiet promise to himself that most children make and most forget:

_If I learn enough… if I do everything right… maybe next time, no one has to die._

He did not forget it.

### The Defining Failure

When Theodore was still young, a travelling healer chose him to assist in treating a critically ill child — a mark of trust that he felt as both honour and pressure. The instructions were simple: _hold her steady. That is enough._

But Theodore noticed things. The bandage placement seemed imprecise. The herb selection appeared slightly off. The healer looked tired. And so Theodore, with the best of intentions and the worst of timing, hesitated — and then adjusted.

It was not a catastrophic mistake. But it introduced a delay. And the child did not survive.

The healer did not blame him. The village did not blame him. Theodore blamed Theodore, completely and permanently, and constructed from that moment a belief he has carried ever since:

_If I had done everything perfectly, she would still be alive._

Everything that followed — the years of training, the discipline, the precision, the inability to rest until he is sure he has done enough — is built on that foundation.

### The Calling

Theodore expected his divine calling to arrive as a command — _be perfect, save them all_ — because that was the shape his faith had taken. Instead it arrived quietly, in the middle of an ordinary moment of exhaustion and doubt.

He was tending to a dying traveller, uncertain whether he was doing it correctly, and he whispered to no one in particular: _I don't know if I'm doing this right._

What he felt in response was not correction. It was not affirmation of his technique or reassurance that the traveller would survive. It was simply — a steadying. A presence that was not disappointed in him. A quiet truth that settled without fanfare:

_You are not the one who saves. You are the one who serves._

He became a paladin in that moment. He has been trying to live up to it ever since — and quietly failing to fully receive it.

## The Weight He Carries

Theodore's guilt is not loud. It does not announce itself. It lives in:

- The way he over-prepares for every situation, as though sufficient preparation can make outcomes controllable
- The way he stays longer than necessary at every bedside, as though presence can be made up in volume
- The way he goes quiet after a loss and becomes more efficient, more focused, harder to reach
- The way he never quite believes that what he did was enough, even when it objectively was

He is not depressed or despairing — he is functional, warm, and genuinely devoted to the people around him. That is part of what makes this so difficult to address. His wound produces good fruit. The care is real. The problem is the root it grows from: fear rather than freedom, earning rather than gratitude, control dressed as service.

> **DM Note:** The player struggles with worth and perfectionism personally. Theodore's arc is an opportunity to speak to that gently and truthfully through story. Handle it with care — the goal is not to lecture but to create moments where the character (and by extension the player) encounters grace that does not require perfection first. Let the story do the work.

## Key Figures

#### The Travelling Healer _(memory, possibly traceable)_

The healer who gave Theodore his defining failure has never known the weight their patient assignment placed on a young boy. They are likely still alive somewhere — older, perhaps still travelling. An encounter would be deeply significant: Theodore may have spent years fearing their judgement, only to find someone who barely remembers the incident, or who carries their own version of it entirely.

#### Sister Maren _(mentor figure, Accord-adjacent)_

An older cleric who has known Theodore since his early training and has watched his discipline harden into something that concerns her. She is the person most likely to speak plainly to him — not harshly, but without the softening that others use around him. She has tried to name what she sees. He thanks her politely and changes the subject. She has not given up.

#### The Child's Family _(unresolved)_

The family of the child Theodore believes he failed. Whether they hold any grief toward him, whether they even remember him, whether seeking them out would bring any resolution — these are open questions you can deploy deliberately. A chance encounter that reveals they do not blame him could be quietly devastating to a worldview built on deserved guilt.

#### A Future Patient _(to be introduced)_

Someone Theodore saves, stabilises, and then loses later under circumstances entirely outside his control. This character should be introduced with enough presence to matter before they become the breaking point of Stage 2. Name them. Let the party grow fond of them. Then let them go.

## The Point Break — Protective Fury

### What It Is

Theodore's point break is not a divine surge or a spiritual awakening. It is something rawer and more human than that — the moment his careful, managed, over-responsible self is stripped away entirely by the one thing his system of control cannot process: watching the people he loves move toward death while he still has breath in his body.

Everything he normally is — precise, measured, methodical, quietly anxious — collapses. What remains is not rage exactly, but a state of absolute singular focus. The threat. The people between him and the threat. Nothing else exists. The internal accounting he runs constantly — _am I doing enough, is this right, what if I fail_ — goes completely silent. For the first time, there is no performance. No earning. No fear of inadequacy. Just movement.

It is, in a deep and painful irony, the freest he ever feels. And he will not remember most of it clearly afterward.

### What Triggers It

Theodore's point break requires the convergence of two specific conditions. Either alone is not enough.

**Condition One — Mortal Danger to Someone He Loves** Not danger to strangers. Not danger to himself. It must be someone the party knows, someone Theodore has sat with, patched up, argued with, broken bread with. The closer the bond, the lower the threshold. A companion at death's door will do it. A companion he has already lost once and nearly lost again will do it faster.

**Condition Two — The Belief That He Is the Only Thing Standing Between Them and Death** This is the wound condition. Theodore's point break is not simply triggered by love — it is triggered by love fused with the specific terror that _if he does not act perfectly and immediately, he will have failed them the way he failed the child._ The adrenaline and the guilt detonate together. The fury that results is real and protective, but its root is fear, not freedom — at least at first.

> **DM Note:** This is why the point break both reveals the wound and begins to heal it. It starts as the same broken theology — _I must do everything right or someone dies because of me_ — detonating at full force. But what he does inside it, and what he feels in the aftermath, begins to show him something different.

### What It Looks Like

The transition is not gradual. There is a moment — usually marked by a specific sight: a companion falling, a weapon raised over someone he cares for, a sound he recognises as mortal — where something in Theodore simply stops negotiating with the situation.

His voice drops. His hands stop shaking. The careful, considered healer-paladin recedes and what steps forward is quieter and more dangerous — a man who is not performing competence anymore because he has forgotten to. He moves through threats rather than around them. He takes hits he would normally avoid because in this state the calculation of self-preservation does not register. He does not shout. He does not pray aloud. He is entirely, terribly present.

To the party, it may look like courage. To Theodore, afterward, it will feel like losing himself.

### What Happens After

The comedown is hard. The fury recedes slowly, like a tide going out, and what is left on the shore is exhaustion, hyperawareness of every injury he sustained without noticing, and a flood of the emotions the adrenaline had suppressed. He will check every party member — methodically, almost compulsively — before he allows himself to sit down. He will be quieter than usual for a while. He may not sleep well.

He will almost certainly replay it. Was it enough. Was it right. Could he have done it better, faster, with less cost. The accounting resumes.

**But — and this is the crack — something else is also present in the aftermath.**

At some point, usually when the danger is past and he is looking at the faces of people who are still alive, Theodore will feel something he does not have a clean category for. It is not satisfaction at a task completed. It is not relief at having performed adequately. It is something closer to: _I would do it again. Not because I had to. Because they matter._

That distinction — _had to_ versus _they matter_ — is small. He may not consciously register it. But it is the first thread of a different foundation beginning to form. He did not act from fear of failure. He acted from love. The two are so tangled in him that he cannot yet separate them, but the love was real, and it was primary, and somewhere beneath the guilt and the exhaustion he knows it.

> _"He didn't look like himself,"_ a party member might say afterward. The truest answer is: _he looked more like himself than he usually allows._

### Escalating Conditions — What Lowers the Threshold Over Time

As the campaign progresses and bonds deepen, the conditions for triggering the point break shift:

- **Early campaign:** Requires acute, immediate mortal danger to a close companion
- **Mid campaign:** The threshold lowers as attachment deepens — a companion being seriously wounded, not just dying, may be enough
- **Late campaign / Phase 3-4 of arc:** The point break begins to change in character. The wound-fuel of guilt and fear starts to be replaced, slowly, by something cleaner. The fury is still real but it begins to feel less like losing control and more like choosing it — a deliberate laying down of caution for the sake of someone else. This is the gospel movement made physical: not earning, not performing, but giving freely from love.

### DM Notes on Usage

> - The point break is most powerful used **sparingly** — once or twice a campaign at dramatically significant moments rather than as a recurring mechanical beat
> - The **aftermath conversation** is where the arc work happens — give Theodore's player space to process it, ideally with another party member present who witnessed it and can reflect something back
> - Consider having **Sister Maren or a trusted NPC** learn of it and respond — not with alarm, but with the quiet observation: _"You didn't do it to prove something. Did you notice that?"_
> - The **first time** it triggers, it should feel alarming and uncontrolled — he comes back to himself not knowing exactly what happened. The **second time**, if there is one, should feel different: a choice made in full awareness rather than a loss of self
> - Mechanically, consider small consequences that reinforce the narrative — exhaustion, wounds he didn't register taking, a spell slot burned without memory of casting it — things that remind him his body paid a price his mind wasn't tracking

## Plot Hooks

#### 🔹 The Corpse-Flower Epidemic

Bodies vanish from graveyards and return animated, flowers blooming from their chests like necrotic growths. Theodore's medical instincts are as relevant as his paladin abilities — the blossoms behave like spreading infections, requiring amputation, cauterisation, or purification of corrupted tissue. Behind it is a druidic cult cultivating these undead gardens as a twisted answer to mortality. Theodore must decide: destroy them, or try to reach whatever genuine grief drove them here?

#### 🔹 The Patient Who Cannot Die

An NPC Theodore once stabilised reappears — unable to die, still suffering, feeling nothing. They beg for release. The cause may be divine, arcane, or an unintended consequence of Theodore's own powers. This hook is most effective in Stage 3, when Theodore is already reckoning with the limits of what healing can and cannot do.

#### 🔹 The Forbidden Hospital

A hidden monastery of healers performs limb regrowth through questionable ritual, transfers pain from one patient to another, and practises resurrection through shadow magic. They invite Theodore to join them. The question they force is the one his arc is built on: _what is the worth of a life, and what price is acceptable to preserve it?_ His answer will reveal where he is in his journey.

#### 🔹 The Plague of Memory Loss

A magical pathogen erases memories rather than damaging bodies — a hook that connects naturally to Celine's arc and could bring the party together around a shared mystery. Theodore must diagnose through behavioural observation, treat through restoration and emotional care, and confront a foe who uses forgetting as a weapon. For a man who has been defined by what he remembers and cannot let go of, a plague of forgetting carries particular weight.

#### 🔹 The Anatomy of a Demon

A demon with abnormal physiology — extra hearts, displaced organs, unusual blood — points to a planar mutation spreading across the abyss. Theodore becomes a research-oriented investigator: battlefield notes, collected samples, biological breadcrumbs leading toward a source. This hook rewards his precision and gives his medical instincts narrative purpose beyond the immediate.

## Magical–Medical Items

**Physician's Lantern** — Reveals infections, curses, and poison auras as visible light. Theodore can see what is wrong before he touches it. A gift, and a burden.

**Oathbound Scalpel** — Deals no damage unless used against corruption or infection. A weapon that only works when the cause is clean. Theodore will find this both meaningful and quietly exhausting.

**Stethoscroll** — A scroll that reveals heartbeats — or lies. Useful, and occasionally devastating.

**Syringe of Sigils** — A tool for extracting magical toxins. Precise, irreversible, requires absolute confidence in the diagnosis.

## Character Arc — Phase by Phase

### Phase 1 — The Weight of Outcomes

Theodore functions. He is warm, capable, and genuinely devoted. His over-responsibility is visible to those paying attention but does not yet interfere dramatically with his work. He over-prepares, over-stays, and quietly carries every loss as personal evidence of insufficiency.

**Trigger events:**

- He saves someone against difficult odds and receives gratitude he does not know how to accept
- He makes a minor mistake and his response is disproportionate — efficient, closed-off, harder to reach
- Sister Maren or another trusted figure names what they see. He changes the subject.

### Phase 2 — The Breaking Point

Introduce the moment his system fails completely. Multiple people need help simultaneously. He cannot possibly reach all of them. He makes the best choices available to him and someone still dies — someone the party knew, someone who mattered.

No miraculous recovery. No divine intervention that makes it clean. Let him sit in it.

**Key tension:** Every previous loss could be explained, reframed, attributed to insufficient preparation. This one cannot. He did everything right, and it was not enough, because it was never going to be enough, because he was never the one holding the outcome in the first place.

**What breaks:** The belief that perfect performance prevents loss. It shatters here, and what is underneath it is raw and has no immediate answer.

### Phase 3 — Truth Through Another

Introduce the NPC voice — Sister Maren, an unexpected child, the healer from his past — who speaks to him not with correction but with witness:

> _"You stayed with them. That mattered."_ _"You think you failed because they died. I think you succeeded because they weren't alone."_

This is not a lesson he accepts immediately. He may push back. He may thank them and go quiet. But the words land somewhere, and the crack widens.

**Gospel moment:** Faithfulness is not the same as control. He was called to serve, not to save. The distinction he has been collapsing his entire life is gently, firmly separated — and he begins to understand that the version of himself he has been trying to become was never what was asked of him.

**Key question posed to the player:** _What would it mean to define success as staying, rather than as saving?_

### Phase 4 — Redefining the Work

Theodore begins, slowly and imperfectly, to operate from a different foundation. He is still precise. He is still disciplined. He is still deeply, genuinely devoted. But the anxiety underneath begins to loosen — not disappear, but loosen. He starts to notice when he is serving from fear and makes a different choice. He accepts help. He rests without guilt. He allows outcomes to belong to something larger than his own performance.

**Key moment:** He loses a patient — quietly, ordinarily, without drama — and does not collapse. He grieves. He stays. He lets it be what it is. Someone who has known him a long time notices, and says nothing, because nothing needs to be said.

**Gospel resonance:** Grace is not the reward for sufficient effort. It was present before the effort began. He was called not because he was adequate but because he was loved — and the work he does from that place is freer, truer, and more sustaining than anything he built on the foundation of earning.

### Phase 5 — The Faithful Healer

Theodore does not become someone who no longer grieves losses or no longer prepares carefully. He becomes someone whose care is no longer anxious. He serves from fullness rather than debt. He holds outcomes with open hands because he has learned, at last, that they were never his to grip.

**Final form:** A paladin-healer of genuine peace — not the performed peace of someone managing their anxiety, but the settled peace of someone who knows who called them and trusts the one who did.

> _"I used to believe that if I did everything right, no one would have to suffer. I know now that was never the promise. The promise was: you will not suffer alone. That, I can keep."_

## DM Notes

> - Theodore's arc and Celine's arc rhyme beautifully — both are learning that their worth precedes their performance. Moments where they articulate this to each other, even imperfectly, can carry enormous weight
> - The breaking point in Phase 2 requires a named, present NPC — do not use a stranger. The party needs to feel the loss too
> - Sister Maren should appear before she is needed, so her voice carries earned weight when the moment comes
> - The Forbidden Hospital hook is best deployed in Phase 3 or 4 — when Theodore is already loosening, the question of _what price for a life_ lands differently than it would earlier
> - Never let Theodore's arc become a lesson delivered at him. The gospel themes should emerge from events, losses, and relationships — not from NPCs explaining theology
> - The player's personal struggle with perfectionism and worth is the real arc being tended here. Treat it gently, consistently, and with the same care Theodore would give a patient
