## Overview

Celine is a Wood Elf ranger who woke up with dirt on her hands, a blade at her side, and no memory of how either came to be there. She does not know her family, her homeland, or the life she lived before the morning she opened her eyes in a forest clearing and found herself — in every sense — alone. What she does know is this: something in her recognises injustice the way a compass recognises north. She cannot explain it. She does not try to. She simply follows it.

She serves the **Verdant Accord**, an organisation dedicated to upholding justice and peace across the wilder reaches of the world, and she is good at the work — disciplined, perceptive, and quietly relentless. Her identity, for now, is built entirely on what she does rather than who she is. She is a ranger. She protects people. She upholds what is right.

This is enough. Most days, she believes that.

---

## The Gospel Thread — Identity, Grace & Worth

Celine's arc is built around a single quietly devastating question: _if you do not know who you are, how do you know you are worth anything?_

Her answer, at the start of the campaign, is functional but fragile: she earns her worth. Every mission completed, every innocent protected, every wrong made right is another brick in a wall she is building against the void where her identity should be. She is not cruel or joyless — she is steady, and kind, and genuinely good. But her goodness is quietly anxious. She serves because she needs to. Because the moment she stops being useful, she is not sure what is left.

The gospel movement of her arc is the slow, difficult discovery that she was already known — already named, already loved — before she ever drew her bow in anyone's defence. Her worth was never in her deeds. The memory loss is not only a narrative mystery. It is a spiritual one: _can you believe you are beloved when you cannot remember a single person who has ever loved you?_

Her arc does not resolve this question quickly. It lives in it, faithfully, across the campaign.

> _"I do not know what I was. I know what I choose to be. For now, that has to be enough."_

## Origins — What Is Known

Celine is a Wood Elf, bearing the quiet marks of a people shaped by old forests and older silences — keen eyes, unhurried movement, and a relationship with the natural world that feels less like skill and more like memory her body kept when her mind did not.

She was found by a patrol of the Verdant Accord approximately three years before the campaign begins, unconscious at the edge of a forest that showed signs of recent and significant magical disturbance. She carried no identification, no letters, no sigils of house or clan. The blade at her side was well-used and well-maintained. Her hands were calloused in the way of someone who had trained for years. Whatever she had been, she had been doing it seriously.

The Accord took her in. She recovered. She asked questions no one could answer. Eventually, she stopped asking and started working — and found, to her own quiet surprise, that she was very good at it.

She has been with the Accord ever since.

---

## The Verdant Accord

A wide-ranging organisation of rangers, scouts, mediators, and wardens dedicated to keeping peace in the regions beyond city walls — settling disputes between settlements, protecting trade roads, responding to threats that no local authority has the reach to address. They are not soldiers and not quite law enforcement. They occupy the space between.

The Accord operates on a code of conduct built around three commitments: **protect the vulnerable, pursue the truth, and hold to mercy even when justice is hard.** This last principle is the one Celine wrestles with most. Justice is clean. Mercy is complicated. She is still learning that they are not opposites.

The Accord knows Celine's situation. Her commanding officer, **Warden Saethis**, took a personal interest in her case when she arrived and has quietly maintained it ever since — logging details, following cold leads, protecting her from pressure to explain herself to those who would use her uncertainty against her. He believes she deserves to find the truth. He also suspects the truth may be more dangerous than she realises.

## The Memory Loss — The Central Mystery

Celine's memory does not return gradually. It returns in fragments — sensory and disorienting rather than clear. A smell that makes her stop walking. A melody she finds herself humming without knowing where she learned it. The specific way candlelight on stone feels like grief.

What she has pieced together so far amounts to very little: she was trained somewhere cold and disciplined. She has a scar on her left forearm that looks deliberate rather than accidental — a mark, not a wound. She sometimes wakes from dreams in which she is standing in a hall of some kind, listening to someone speak, and she knows the words matter enormously, and she can never quite hear them.

### The Truth — DM Eyes Only

Celine was a member of a now-fractured elven order called the **Aureate Vigil** — a fellowship of rangers and wardens who served as peacekeepers and truth-seekers in the elven highlands, bound together by a shared oath and a shared faith in a set of ancient principles that read, to any careful reader, like a natural law written into the world itself.

The Vigil was destroyed from within. A senior member — **Archkeeper Vauren** — had grown convinced that true justice required the removal of mercy as a limiting principle. That the Vigil's restraint was weakness. That peace enforced by the willing tolerance of evil was not peace at all. He was not entirely wrong, which made him very dangerous.

Celine opposed him. She was one of a small group who tried to stop what Vauren was building before it consumed the order entirely. She failed — or nearly did. The night the Vigil fractured, something happened that she does not remember: a confrontation, a choice, a cost she paid that has never been recovered.

She did not lose her memories by accident. She lost them because someone, in an act of desperate and possibly misguided love, chose to take them from her rather than let her carry what she had witnessed. Who made that choice, and whether it was merciful or a violation, is one of the arc's central unresolved questions.

Vauren and the remnants of his faction still exist — calling themselves the **Ironvigil** now, pursuing their vision of absolute justice without mercy, and aware that at least one witness to their origins is still walking the world.

The scar on her forearm is the Vigil's oath mark. Anyone who knows what it is will recognise it.

## Key Figures

#### Warden Saethis _(Verdant Accord, commanding officer)_

A human warden in his late fifties who has seen enough of the world to be neither surprised nor cynical about it. He took Celine in without demanding explanations she couldn't give, and has been a steady, quiet presence in her life since. He does not father her — he respects her too much for that. But he watches. And he worries. He has recently come across a record that mentions the Aureate Vigil and a ranger matching Celine's description, and he is deciding carefully how and when to bring it to her.

#### Archkeeper Vauren _(Ironvigil, primary antagonist)_

An older elf of genuine conviction and genuine danger. He is not a man who does evil for pleasure or power — he does it because he has constructed a moral framework in which it is necessary. He believes mercy is a lie the comfortable tell themselves, and he has built an organisation on that belief. He remembers Celine. He respects her, in his way. He will try to recruit her before he tries to destroy her — which is more unsettling than the alternative.

#### The One Who Took Her Memories _(identity withheld — reveal TBD)_

Someone who loved Celine enough to do something terrible to protect her. Their identity, their reasons, and whether what they did was an act of grace or a violation of her autonomy is the arc's deepest moral question. They are likely still alive. They have likely been watching from a distance. They do not know how to approach her now.

#### A Fellow Vigil Survivor _(identity flexible — DM to define)_

At least one other member of the original Aureate Vigil survived and is living under a different name. They will recognise Celine before she recognises them. What they choose to do in that moment — run, warn her, or finally tell her the truth — depends on how events have unfolded and how safe they feel.

## Character Arc — Phase by Phase

### Phase 1 — Competence as Identity

Celine defines herself entirely by her work. She is steady, capable, and quietly driven. Her fear of the memory void is managed through action — every mission completed is another answer to the question she cannot otherwise resolve. She does not speak of the void often. She functions, and functioning feels like enough.

But underneath the discipline is a fragile theology: _I am what I do. If I stop doing good, I stop being good. If I cannot remember who I was, I must earn who I am._

**Trigger events:**

- Someone recognises her oath mark and reacts with fear or reverence
- A fragment memory surfaces at a dramatically inconvenient moment
- She is asked directly who she is, and finds she cannot answer
- She fails at something — and discovers how little she knows what to do when she cannot perform her way back to solid ground

### Phase 2 — Cracks in the Foundation

Leads begin to surface. The name Aureate Vigil appears. Someone from her past enters the picture. The framework she has built — _I am what I do_ — begins to buckle under the weight of a past she cannot account for.

**Key tension:** She is afraid that who she was will contradict who she is now. That when the truth arrives, it will not set her free — it will unmake her. She has built an identity out of righteous deeds, and she is beginning to suspect that is not the same as having one.

**Key moment:** She fails to protect someone, or makes a costly mistake, and the question surfaces without warning: _if I am only what I do, and I have done this — what am I now?_ The performance of worth cracks open. The gospel question underneath it is exposed: _is there anything beneath the doing that still has value?_

### Phase 3 — The Truth & The Wound

The Ironvigil makes themselves known. Vauren's offer comes — framed as homecoming, as clarity, as justice finally unshackled from the weakness of mercy. And fragment by fragment, the truth of the Aureate Vigil begins to surface.

Here the arc splits into two simultaneous revelations:

**The external truth:** What the Vigil was. What Vauren did. What Celine opposed and what it cost her. She was not passive in her story — she stood for something, at great personal cost, before she could remember standing for anything.

**The internal truth:** She learns what was taken from her, and by whom, and why. The person who erased her memories did not do so to diminish her. They did it because they loved her and could not bear to watch her carry the weight of what she had seen. This is grace — real, costly, unauthorised, imperfect grace. Someone paid a price on her behalf that she never consented to and cannot repay.

**Gospel moment:** She was known before she could remember being known. She was loved before she could perform being loveable. The past she has been desperately trying to earn her way out of turns out to contain someone who saw her fully and chose her anyway. This is not a comfortable revelation. It is a destabilising, reorienting one — the kind that requires her to rebuild her understanding of worth from the ground up.

**Key question posed to the player:** _If your worth was never in your deeds — if you were already known, already chosen, already loved before you could remember any of it — what does that mean for how you have been living?_

### Phase 4 — Redemption & the Rebuilt Self

This is the hardest phase. Celine must sit with the truth before she can move through it. She knows now who she was. She knows she was loved. She also knows she has spent years performing worth she already had, defending an identity built on sand, and serving from fear rather than freedom.

Redemption here is not dramatic. It is quiet and daily — the slow work of receiving grace she did not earn and cannot repay, and learning to act from that place instead. She begins to serve not because she needs to justify her existence, but because she is secure enough in who she is to give freely.

**Key moment:** She extends mercy to someone who does not deserve it — not because she has weighed the cost and found it acceptable, but because she has been shown unearned grace and it has changed what she believes people are worth.

**Gospel resonance:** She cannot redeem herself. She never could. The arc is not about becoming worthy — it is about discovering she already was, and letting that truth slowly heal the anxious, performing, earning version of herself that she had become.

### Phase 5 — Known, Named & Free

Whether or not her full memories return, Celine arrives at settled identity — not because she recovered the past, but because she has been received by truth and has chosen to stand in it. She knows her name. She knows her worth. She knows that mercy and justice are not opposites — they are the same love, expressed differently depending on what the moment requires.

**Final form:** A ranger who pursues justice without fear and extends mercy without apology. Who serves from fullness rather than lack. Who is, for the first time, quietly and completely herself — not because she earned it, but because she was always already that person, waiting to be found.

> _"I spent three years trying to become someone worth knowing. It turns out I was known before I started."_

Nature: The Failure of Performance

Celine's point break is the moment her carefully constructed identity — _I am what I do, I earn my worth through righteous action_ — is confronted with something it cannot process: a situation where doing everything right is not enough, or where the right thing to do is not clear, or where the cost of acting from her earned-worth theology is paid by someone other than herself.

It does not look like fury. It looks like a woman who has suddenly lost the floor beneath her feet and is still moving because stopping is not something her body knows how to do.

### Trigger Conditions

**Condition One — Her Competence Fails Someone Innocent** Not a tactical error. Not a combat loss. The specific trigger is a moment where Celine did everything correctly — tracked the right target, made the right call, upheld the right principle — and an innocent person suffered for it anyway. The performance was perfect and it was not enough. This is the crack that splits the foundation, because her entire identity is built on the premise that sufficient competence prevents loss.

**Condition Two — Her Identity Is Directly Challenged** The wound condition. This can come from outside — someone who knows what the oath mark on her arm means, someone from the Ironvigil who recognises her, Vauren himself naming her by her Vigil name — or from inside, in a moment where she catches herself acting from fear rather than conviction and cannot immediately correct it. The challenge does not need to be hostile. The truth does not need to be cruel. It simply needs to be true, and it needs to land in a moment when her defences are already thinned by condition one.

### What It Looks Like

Celine does not break loudly. The point break does not produce tears or fury or collapse. What it produces is a kind of terrible clarity — a woman stripped of the performance moving purely on instinct, and discovering, to her own shock, that the instincts are good. That underneath the earned-worth theology is something that does not need to earn anything. Something that acts rightly not because it is keeping score but because it simply cannot do otherwise.

She may push past tactical caution entirely — moving into danger she would normally calculate around, making choices that prioritise people over principles, acting with a sureness that has nothing to do with training. It is only afterward, when the adrenaline recedes, that she realises she does not know where that came from. It did not feel like performance. It felt like herself.

The memory fragments are most likely to surface in the aftermath of a point break — the crack lets light in, and light reaches things that were sealed.

### The Gospel Dimension

Celine's point break is the first evidence, experienced rather than reasoned, that there is something beneath the performance that has value. She acted rightly when the performance framework gave her nothing to work with. That action came from somewhere. It was not earned in that moment — it simply was. This is the crack through which the gospel truth enters: _you were already this person before you had anything to prove it with._

### Escalation

- **Early:** Requires both conditions; the break is brief and she reassembles quickly, attributing it to adrenaline
- **Mid:** The oath mark becomes a more active trigger as the Aureate Vigil thread surfaces; the breaks last longer and the reassembly is slower and less complete
- **Late / Phase 3-4:** The point break transforms — it is no longer a loss of foundation but an experience of a different, truer foundation. She begins to act from it deliberately. The last version of the point break is not a break at all. It is a choice.

## DM Notes

> - The oath mark scar is your best tool for gradual revelation — NPCs who recognise it before Celine understands it give you controlled, dramatic information drops
> - Vauren should never be played as simply evil — the more the party finds him _reasonable_, the more dangerous and thematically resonant he becomes
> - The person who took her memories is your most sensitive narrative charge — handle the reveal with care; it should feel like grace and grief simultaneously
> - The gospel themes work best when they emerge from events rather than dialogue — let the story _demonstrate_ identity and grace rather than having characters explain it
> - Celine's arc pairs naturally with Pumbaa's — both characters are reckoning with a past that was hidden from them, and both are being asked whether truth changes who they are now
