## Overview

Euler is a wizard who does not belong here — not in this era, not in this city, not in this particular tavern arguing about the price of a room he intends to leave without paying. He is sharp, self-contained, and operating at all times with the specific efficiency of a man who has decided that the most important person in any room is himself. He is not cruel for cruelty's sake. He is simply, comprehensively, oriented inward — every calculation, every alliance, every act of apparent generosity pointing back toward his own survival, his own restoration, his own return to a time and place where he made sense.

He is also, though he would not frame it this way, profoundly lost. Not spatially. Temporally, yes, but that is almost beside the point. He is lost in the way of a man who has made himself the centre of everything and discovered that a centre of one is a very small universe to live in.

The gospel question at the heart of his arc is deceptively simple: _what if the life you keep trying to get back to was never going to be enough — and the life in front of you, with these people, in this era, is the one that was always meant for you?_

## The Gospel Thread — From the Inside Out, Turned Outward

Euler's wound is radical self-orientation. It did not begin as selfishness in the petty sense. It began as survival — a man stripped of memory, power, and context, dropped into a world centuries before his birth, with nothing reliable except his own intelligence and will. Turning inward was reasonable. It kept him alive. The problem is that it has become a permanent posture rather than a temporary measure, and Euler has begun — without noticing — to confuse the map for the territory. He is not surviving anymore. He is simply serving himself, because the habit is deep and the alternative has never been presented in a form he could receive.

The scripture that speaks to his arc is not a rebuke. It is an invitation:

> _"For whoever would save his life will lose it, but whoever loses his life for my sake will find it."_ — Matthew 16:25

Euler has been saving his life with both hands since the moment he arrived. He has been very good at it. He has also, quietly and without fully registering it, been losing something in the process — the capacity for connection, for investment, for the specific fullness that comes from giving rather than accumulating. His arc is not a moral lecture about selfishness. It is the slow, sometimes unwilling discovery that a life turned outward — toward these specific, inconvenient, irreplaceable people — is larger and truer than the life he has been so carefully preserving.

## Era of Origin — The Crystal Horizon

> _Full DM detail — player knows only fragments_

Euler originates from an era known among its inhabitants as **The Crystal Horizon** — a mage-engineered civilisation centuries beyond the current Forgotten Realms timeline, characterised by the seamless fusion of arcane theory and technological application. In this era:

- **Arcanotech** has replaced most mundane technology — communication, transport, medicine, and warfare are all channelled through spell-integrated systems
- **Temporal studies** are an established but classified magical discipline, practiced openly only by licensed Chronomancers under strict regulatory frameworks
- **Governing councils** of Chronomancers, Arcanists, and Technomancers administrate society with precision and, depending on which faction you ask, either admirable order or suffocating control
- **Time travel** is possible, tightly restricted, and the subject of the most serious legal penalties the era can produce

Euler was, in this era, a Technomancer of significant standing — a practitioner who worked at the intersection of arcane theory and engineered systems, able to interface with arcanotech infrastructure in ways most wizards could not. He was brilliant, ambitious, and had acquired the specific arrogance of someone who has been told he is exceptional for long enough that he has stopped questioning whether exceptional comes with obligations.

He cannot remember most of this. What he experiences are impressions — the texture of surfaces that do not exist in this era, the instinctive reach for interfaces that are not there, the faint ghost of a methodology that leaks into his spellcasting as something the people around him cannot quite categorise.

## The Equilibrium

> _DM Eyes Only — Euler has only the sigil and a bone-deep dread_

The Equilibrium is the clandestine order that polices temporal integrity in the Crystal Horizon — officially nonexistent, practically omnipresent, answerable to no government and bound by a code of temporal ethics so classified that most Chronomancers have never read it in full.

Their mandate: identify and neutralise temporal anomalies before they cascade. This includes paradoxes, timeline contamination, and — most relevantly — individuals whose future actions have been assessed as posing unacceptable risk to temporal stability.

The Equilibrium does not punish retroactively. They act pre-emptively, which means Euler was exiled for something he had not yet done at the time of his exile — something his future self did, or was going to do, that The Equilibrium determined could not be permitted.

**The sigil burned into his memory:** A circle, nested within a triangle, crossed by a single horizontal line. He doodles it without knowing why. He has never seen it in this era. He does not know he is looking for it.

## What Euler Actually Did — The Hidden Truth

> _The campaign's deepest secret — reveal in Phase 3-4_

Euler did not act out of malice. He acted out of the same inward orientation that defines him now — but at catastrophic scale.

In the Crystal Horizon's future, Euler discovered something: a temporal convergence point, a moment in history where the timeline was uniquely malleable. He intended to use it — not to destroy anything, but to rewrite a specific loss. Someone he cared about. Perhaps the only person he ever turned outward toward, and lost. He intended to go back and prevent it.

The Equilibrium's models showed that the intervention, while locally successful, would create a cascade that unmade three centuries of history and approximately forty million lives downstream. Euler knew this. He proceeded anyway — or was going to. Whether he actually did it, was stopped mid-attempt, or was exiled in the moment of decision is deliberately ambiguous until the reveal.

The crime, in other words, was this: _he was willing to sacrifice the world for one person._ The dark mirror of love. Devotion turned inward and then detonated outward at catastrophic cost.

When he understands this, it will break something open in him. The man who could sacrifice forty million strangers for one person he loved is not a monster. He is someone who finally felt something for another person so strongly that it consumed his ethics entirely. His arc is the discovery that this capacity — to feel that strongly, to love that specifically — is real and good and his. It simply needs to be turned outward rather than weaponised.

> **DM Note:** The person he tried to save should be introduced carefully — a name in a recovered memory, a face in a dream, someone who is either an ancestor of a current NPC or connected to the campaign's world in a way that makes their existence feel real. Do not reveal this too early. It should arrive when Euler has already begun to turn outward toward the party — so that the revelation reframes his capacity for love rather than simply explaining his crime.

## The Exile & His Current State

The temporal banishment ritual used by The Equilibrium does not simply move a person through time. It fragments them — strips away memories, severs emotional connections, corrupts spell knowledge, and deposits what remains into the target era with just enough coherence to survive. The intention is not death. The intention is removal: take the dangerous element out of the equation and scatter enough of it that reassembly is practically impossible.

Euler has been reassembling anyway. He is stubborn in the specific way of someone who has always believed that sufficient intelligence applied with sufficient will can solve any problem. He is not wrong that this has worked before. He is wrong that it is sufficient for this.

**Current magical presentation:** His spellcasting is recognisably wizard-class but with anomalies — effects that arrive at slightly wrong angles, interfaces with magical systems that show an intuition for underlying structure no medieval-era wizard should have, occasional terminology that belongs to no known school. Other wizards find him unsettling. He does not explain himself.

**What he carries:** The sigil, doodled on anything available. A fragment of something that functions like a pocket calculating device — inert, unrecognisable to anyone in this era, occasionally warm. A methodology of problem-solving that is centuries more advanced than his apparent expertise. A dread he cannot source and has learned not to examine.

## Character & Presentation

Euler presents as shrewd, self-contained, and faintly superior — not in a theatrical way, but in the specific way of someone who has genuinely concluded that he is operating at a higher level than most of the people around him and sees no reason to pretend otherwise. He is not wrong about his intelligence. He is wrong about what intelligence is for.

He is capable of warmth in the way of someone who has the social skill to produce it when it serves a purpose. What he is not yet capable of — or not yet willing to be — is warmth that costs him something with no return. Genuine warmth. Warmth that simply is, because the other person matters.

He will develop this. It will be inconvenient for him. He will resist it. It will happen anyway, because the people around him are genuinely worth caring about, and Euler is not nearly as defended as he believes.

## Key Figures

#### The Person He Tried To Save _(identity withheld — DM to define)_

Someone from his past in the Crystal Horizon — a colleague, a mentor, a companion — whose death or loss was the fracture point that turned his self-orientation into something catastrophic. He does not remember them clearly. He remembers the shape of the loss: a specific weight, an absence where something was. When the memory returns fully, it will not justify what he did. It will explain it, which is more complicated and more important.

#### An Equilibrium Agent In This Era _(to be introduced)_

The Equilibrium does not only operate in the Crystal Horizon. They have placed observers at key temporal convergence points throughout history — quiet, long-lived figures whose job is to watch for anomalies and report. One of them is aware of Euler's presence in this era. They have not yet moved. They are watching. Whether they are sent to retrieve him, eliminate him, or simply observe depends on what Euler does — and whether his actions in this era constitute new risk or accidental correction.

#### A Party Member Who Refuses To Be Instrumentalised _(most likely Theodore or Celine)_

At some point, Euler will attempt to use a party member as a resource in service of his own goals — not maliciously, just efficiently. The party member in question will refuse, or will comply and be hurt by it, and will say something honest about what happened. This is Euler's first real encounter with the cost of self-orientation paid by someone else. It will be uncomfortable. It will matter.

## Technomancer Flavour — Mechanical Suggestions

Euler's future-origin leaks into his spellcasting in ways that are flavourful rather than mechanically game-breaking:

- **Spell descriptions** carry technical language — he describes _Fireball_ as a "thermic radial discharge," _Detect Magic_ as "running a field scan," _Counterspell_ as "jamming the signal"
- **Problem-solving** reflects systems thinking — he approaches dungeons, puzzles, and social situations with an analytical framework that occasionally produces brilliant solutions and occasionally misses the obvious human element entirely
- **Arcanotech fragments** surface occasionally as flavour — he instinctively reaches for interfaces that are not there, references tools that do not exist, occasionally draws diagrams of devices that will not be invented for centuries
- **Spell irregularities** — at DM discretion, spells occasionally manifest with slight anachronistic flavour: a _Shield_ spell that looks like a hard-light barrier rather than a magical ward, a _Mage Hand_ that moves with mechanical precision rather than magical fluidity

## Character Arc — Phase by Phase

### Phase 1 — The Efficient Self

Euler functions on pure self-interest dressed as pragmatism. He is useful to the party because being useful is tactically sound — it keeps him alive, resourced, and positioned. He makes the right moves for the wrong reasons, and the gap between the two is invisible from the outside.

**Trigger events:**

- He makes a calculated decision that benefits himself at mild cost to a party member — and it works, and he feels nothing about the cost
- Someone thanks him genuinely for something he did for entirely selfish reasons — and the gratitude lands in a way he did not expect and cannot immediately process
- A memory fragment surfaces — not the big one, but a small one. A sound. A name said in a specific voice. He doodles the sigil immediately afterward without noticing.

### Phase 2 — The Cost Becomes Visible

The self-orientation begins to have costs that are harder to ignore — not to Euler, but to the people around him, and Euler is too observant to miss it. He does not immediately change. But he begins to calculate the social and relational cost of his choices with a precision he did not previously apply, because something in him has started to register the people around him as variables that matter beyond their utility.

**Key tension:** He begins to notice that he anticipates the party's wellbeing — not because it serves him, but because something has shifted in the function. They have become people to him rather than resources. He is irritated by this. It is inefficient.

**Key moment:** He does something for a party member that costs him something real — time, resources, a tactical advantage — with no calculable return. He does not announce it. He may not even fully acknowledge it to himself. But it happened. Something is turning.

### Phase 3 — The Memory Returns

A significant memory fragment breaks through — enough to show him the shape of what he did, if not all the details. He knows he did something catastrophic. He knows it was for someone he loved. He does not yet know the scale.

**Gospel moment:** He is forced to reckon with the paradox at the centre of his arc: the one time he turned outward — toward another person, with genuine love — he destroyed everything. No wonder he turned back inward. No wonder self-orientation felt safe. But the answer to love-that-destroys is not the absence of love. It is love rightly ordered — turned outward toward the many rather than seized for the one.

**Key question for the player:** _If the most destructive thing you ever did came from love, does that mean love is dangerous — or that love without wisdom, love turned inward and possessive, is the dangerous thing?_

### Phase 4 — The Outward Turn

Euler begins, with characteristic reluctance and characteristic intelligence, to apply his considerable gifts toward something other than his own restoration. Not because he has abandoned the goal of returning home — but because the party has become real enough to him that he cannot treat them as expendable en route to it.

This is the arc's inflection point. The man who was willing to sacrifice forty million strangers for one person begins to discover that he would sacrifice something of himself for six specific people who are right in front of him. The capacity was always there. It was aimed wrong.

**Key moment:** He makes a choice that costs him a genuine opportunity to recover his power or his past — in order to protect or serve the party. He does not explain it. He deflects if asked. The party notices anyway.

**Gospel resonance:** _Whoever loses his life will find it._ Euler has been saving his life with both hands. The moment he begins to release it — toward others, toward the present, toward the inconvenient reality that these people matter more than his timeline — something expands in him that all his self-preservation never produced.

### Phase 5 — Present Tense

Euler does not stop wanting to understand his exile. He does not stop being shrewd, or calculative, or faintly superior. He becomes, however, a person for whom the present is not merely a waiting room for the future he is trying to recover. He is here. These are his people. Whatever he did, whatever he was, whatever is coming — he is here now, and here turns out to be where the fullest version of his life is located.

Whether he ever returns to the Crystal Horizon is a question for the end of the campaign. The more important question — the one his arc answers — is whether he would still choose to, if it meant leaving this behind.

> _"I came here to find my way back. I did not expect to find something worth staying for."_

## Limit Break

### Nature: The Calculation Fails

Euler's limit break is the moment his self-oriented calculus simply cannot produce an answer — where the variables are people he has admitted matter to him, and no configuration of outcomes serves both his interests and theirs, and he must choose. He chooses them. It is the most irrational thing he has ever done. It is also, unmistakably, the most him.

### Trigger Conditions

**Condition One — The Party Is In Danger He Could Have Prevented** Not general danger — the specific trigger is a situation where Euler's prior self-orientation created or worsened the risk. A piece of information he withheld. An alliance he did not make because it did not serve him. A moment where the bill for his self-interest comes due and the party is the one paying it. The guilt is new and does not fit anywhere in his existing framework.

**Condition Two — The Rational Move Is To Walk Away** The wound condition. The limit break fires specifically when the most tactically sound option — the option his pre-arc self would have taken without hesitation — is to cut his losses and preserve himself. He does not take it. He does not take it because the people in danger are not variables anymore, and he cannot make the calculation that would require them to be.

### What It Looks Like

Euler stops calculating. This is, for someone who has been running calculations continuously since he arrived in this era, visible. The precise, analytical quality of his attention shifts into something more direct and considerably more dangerous. He does not become emotional in the dramatic sense. He becomes clear — with the specific clarity of someone who has stopped weighing options because the decision has already been made somewhere beneath the weighing.

His spellcasting in this state loses its measured precision and gains something else: power that pulls from the future-self he was, the full Technomancer rather than the fragmented exile. Spells manifest with Crystal Horizon aesthetics — hard-light constructs, arcanotech visual language, effects that no wizard of this era should be able to produce. It will be disorienting to everyone present, including Euler. The power was always there. The self-orientation was suppressing it.

Afterward, he will be exhausted in a way that is not purely physical, and he will have significantly fewer defences against the question of what just happened. This is the moment a party member should sit with him. Not to debrief. Just to be present. It is the thing he is learning to receive.

### Gospel Dimension

Euler's limit break is the moment he loses his life in order to find it. He releases the self-protective calculus entirely — throws himself toward people rather than away from risk — and discovers that the self that remains on the other side of that release is larger, not smaller, than the one he was protecting. The power that surfaces is not new. It was always his. It simply could not come through a posture of constant self-preservation.

## DM Notes

> - Euler's arc is the most intellectually framed of the party's — his player is likely to engage with the gospel themes through reason before feeling. That is fine. Plant intellectual seeds that have emotional detonators. Let him think his way to the edge, then let experience push him over.
> - **The Equilibrium agent** should be introduced as a background presence long before being identified — a face seen twice, a question asked in a specific way, an NPC who seems to know slightly more than they should.
> - **The fragment device he carries** can be used as a pacing tool — it activates slightly in proximity to temporal anomalies, old magic, or significant convergence points, giving Euler (and you) a way to flag locations of arc significance without exposition.
> - **His spellcasting anomalies** are your best tool for building mystery around his origin — use them sparingly but consistently, and let other characters notice and comment without getting explanations.
> - **The person he tried to save** should be seeded early and revealed late — a recurring name in memory fragments, a face in dreams, a voice associated with the weight of loss. Do not name them until Euler has already begun to turn outward. The revelation should reframe his capacity for love, not excuse his crime.
> - Euler and Zaptos share a dynamic worth cultivating — two wizards, one from the future, one without direction, both discovering that their gifts are for more than they have been using them for. Their conversations could be the most intellectually alive at the table.
> - **The player's personal struggle** between God and worldly distraction maps directly onto Euler's orientation. The arc does not lecture. It simply keeps placing the fullness of an outward life in front of him until he cannot look away from it.
