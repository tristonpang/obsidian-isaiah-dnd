## Overview

Polianthes — Pols to those few he permits the familiarity — is a Dhampir of approximately 200 years, though he carries the weight of centuries that his mind was never allowed to keep. He is the youngest noble of House Brassavola, sole heir of the ruined province of Cuneilabia, and a survivor of horrors so complete that his own grandfather chose mercy over memory and took the worst of them away. He moves through the world refined, unhurried, and faintly melancholic — a man of impeccable taste and deliberate distances, who tends his greenhouse with more tenderness than he extends to most people, and who has joined a party of adventurers for reasons he cannot yet articulate even to himself.

He is not cold. He is careful. There is a difference, and it matters.

> _"I have seen the world: kings and fools, kingdoms, empires and gods. Their rise, their golden shimmering eras and their disastrous declines… I did not relish it."_

## The Gospel Thread — Being Found & Restored

Polianthes' wound is absence. Not dramatic, raging grief — just the quiet, persistent ache of reaching for something that should be there and finding nothing. He cannot remember his parents' faces. He cannot remember his childhood. He knows their scents — amberwood and roses — and a faint melody his father used to hum, and nothing else. He has spent two centuries being gracious about this in public and saying very little about it in private.

What he does not know is that he was loved extravagantly before he could remember being loved at all. That his grandfather wept over him. That his aunt raced across provinces holding him together. That his family dismantled a greenhouse in the rain to find the scent that would reach him when nothing else could. That someone paid an enormous cost — the removal of his memories — not out of carelessness but out of a love that could not bear to watch him suffer.

The gospel arc of his story is the slow discovery that he was known, chosen, and fought for before he could do anything to deserve it. That the people who shaped him did so out of love he cannot remember receiving. And that the party he has inexplicably joined may be the first community in two centuries that has the potential to be, as Saussurea once was — people whose presence reaches him somewhere beneath the careful surface.

## Full Name & Heritage

**Polianthes Brassavola ex domo Nodosa** — _The Brassavola of Nodosa._

The House of Brassavola is one of the oldest noble houses of pureblood vampires, ruling one of the 21 regions of the Land of Ochidaceae. His father, Phyllum, was Count of Cuneilabia — the third of four branches of the House — based in the city of Nodosa. His mother, Polian Rosa, was a human Druid of exceptional skill, renowned as a healer who worked with herbs, plants, and nature.

The name Polianthes was his father's choice — named after his mother, in the way that only a man thoroughly, quietly besotted would name his child.

**Species:** Dhampir — born of pureblood vampire father and human mother **Class:** Druid — his mother's inheritance **Rank:** The Young Lord — sole heir of Cuneilabia **Age:** Approximately 200 years, though he does not look past 30

## House Brassavola — The Family

#### The Duke of Brassavola: Cestrum Brassavola ex domo Cucullata _(Grandfather)_

The patriarch. A diplomat and political operator of long standing, one of the Lords-in-waiting to the Royal household. Composed to the point of seeming untouchable — and yet the only person recorded to have wept openly over Polianthes was Cestrum, alone in a room with a sleeping boy whose memories he was about to take. He plays a formidable hand of Knightschess. He does not discuss what he did that day, and never will.

#### The Marchioness of Sessilabia: Saussurea Brassavola ex domo Augustata _(Aunt)_

Eldest daughter of Cestrum. Oversees the most cities and towns among the siblings, managing all incoming and outgoing trade across Brassavola and beyond. She is as beautiful as she is capable — and she is exceptionally capable. It was Saussurea who found Polianthes in the enemy camp, barely alive, clutching his father's vial. It was Saussurea whose scent — unknowingly carrying traces of the vial she had returned — reached Polianthes when nothing else had. She visits when she can. She writes when she cannot. She watches him with the specific grief of someone who remembers everything he does not.

#### The Count of Cuneilabia: Phyllum Brassavola ex domo Nodosa _(Father, deceased)_

Eldest son of Cestrum. A scholar and linguist, deeply fluent in many languages, meticulous with correspondence, and quietly passionate about vampiric herbology — the greenhouse at the Mansion of the Moon in Nodosa was his. He met Polian Rosa in the woods during a rainstorm, wounded and stumbling, guided to her by the fragrance of a charmed white flower in her hair. He died on the fourth day of the Siege of Subulifolia, having spent three days bleeding himself dry to hold a protective shield around the citizens he could not otherwise save — believing, wrongly, that his son was already dead.

#### The Count of Lateraliflorae: Datura Brassavola ex domo Acaulis _(Uncle)_

Youngest son of Cestrum. Charming, irreverent, dangerous in equal measure — his city of Acaulis sits atop one of the seven gold mines in Ochidaceae, and he manages both the production of coinage and the consolidation of House Brassavola's wealth. He does not have his siblings' academic brilliance, but he has the Midas touch and the combat instinct to back it up. He was the one who ran from the tent screaming for a healer. He does not speak of that day either, but he checks on Polianthes more often than his demeanour would suggest.

## Parents

#### Phyllum Brassavola ex domo Nodosa

A bookworm by temperament and a count by inheritance. He kept a vast greenhouse of rare and exotic plants at his mansion in Nodosa — the Mansion of the Moon — and corresponded in a dozen languages with equal fluency. He smelled of amberwood. He hummed when he was concentrating. He loved his wife with the particular devotion of a man who had not expected to find someone like her, and he died believing his son was gone.

#### Polian Rosa

A human Druid of genuine gift — healer, herbalist, nature-speaker. She wore a charmed sprig of white flower in her hair that retained its fragrance even without roots or leaves. She died at the front lines of the Great War of 82, serving as a healer. She smelled of roses. She placed her protection charm on her son before she died — and that charm kept Polianthes alive through sunlight torture that should have killed him.

Her protection held until the moment he was safe. Then it crumbled to ash, as all completed charms do.

## Childhood — What Polianthes Remembers

Very little. A faint melody his father used to hum. The scent of amberwood and roses. Being led into a great dark hall at twelve years old to meet his grandfather. From there: tutors and governesses, occasional visits from his aunt who taught him trade and languages, his uncle who taught him accounting and metalwork, his grandfather who taught him Knightschess and the grand organ. The Mansion of the Moon in Nodosa exists in his memory as a concept rather than a place — he knows it was his father's, but he has no images of it.

He knows his parents died in the siege of 85. He knows nothing of what preceded that, and nothing of what followed it until he was twelve.

## Childhood — What Actually Happened

> _This is the campaign's central hidden truth for Polianthes. Reveal with great care._

The Great War of 82 reached Cuneilabia in 85 and destroyed the city of Nodosa. Polian died at the front lines. Phyllum led the remaining population toward the citadel of Subulifolia — a city of scholars with only light troops, wholly insufficient for a full siege. During the night exodus, Polianthes' governess was ambushed and killed. Polianthes was taken hostage by enemy troops.

Phyllum, having heard the taunts of the enemy soldiers parading the heads of fallen Nodosians — Polian's among them, and believing his son's head to be there too — was utterly broken. He climbed to the top of the citadel, opened his veins, and used his blood to form a protective shield around the remaining citizens. For three days he held it. On the fourth, Saussurea and Datura arrived with reinforcements and broke the siege. Phyllum died in his sister's arms, too weakened and too grief-shattered to hold on.

Polianthes was not dead. His captors, discovering he could not be killed by sunlight due to his mother's protection charm, subjected him to over a year of sustained torture — fire, sunlight, and cruelty for its own sake — in the northern camp of Cuneilabia. When Saussurea swept through the camp in 86, she found him in the far corner of a tent: burnt, bruised, barely conscious, with a death grip on his father's sapphire vial of amberwood.

She recognised the vial. She had given it to Phyllum herself.

The recovery was slow, harrowing, and incomplete. When Polianthes finally stabilised enough to be moved, he was brought to the Castle of Brassavola. Upon waking, he entered a berserk state — thrashing, screaming, tearing open his healing wounds — unreachable by anyone except Saussurea, whose scent (carrying traces of the amberwood vial) was the only thing that broke through.

After months of failed recovery, when the supply of the restorative scent ran out and Polianthes continued to deteriorate — unable to eat, unable to sleep, disappearing to corners of the castle to bang his head against the walls and weep — Cestrum made the decision. He obtained Brugmansia. He removed the traumatic memories entirely, sealing the boy's soul within the vial to preserve his life while the procedure was done.

It was, depending on how you look at it, an act of profound mercy or a profound violation. Both things are true. Cestrum wept while he did it. It was the only time anyone in the family had ever seen him cry.

Two years later, Polianthes woke with no memory of his first twelve years — only his parents' names, their scents, and the knowledge that they had died in the siege. No portraits of Phyllum or Polian survived the war.

## The Vial

The sapphire vial — cut from a single stone and decorated with gold filigree — contains a blend of amberwood oil and the essence of his mother's charmed flower, recreated by Saussurea, Datura, and the head healer from what remained of Phyllum's greenhouse. It hangs at Polianthes' neck at all times.

It is also a sealed container for his soul, placed there by Cestrum to anchor him during the memory removal. It is, in ways Polianthes does not fully understand, deeply bound to him.

When Pumbaa touched it, something reacted — violently. The vial shattered. The memories inside it — not all of them, but fragments, impressions, the sensory residue of things Polianthes was never supposed to feel again — broke through.

> **DM Note:** The vial's destruction is a major narrative turning point. The sealed memories are no longer fully contained. They will surface — in fragments, in dreams, in moments of acute sensory trigger — with increasing frequency. This is the mechanism by which Polianthes' past will return to him, and it is also deeply, personally traumatic. Handle the aftermath with care.

## Appearance & Character

Polianthes stands at 170cm — his growth stunted by the torture he endured and does not remember. He has long, dark hair, usually kept back, and purple irises that shift to red after blood consumption. His features are youthful in the way of someone who stopped ageing before thirty and has been quietly surprised about it ever since. He favours fine silks and carefully chosen dress, wearing lightweight leather armour only when travel or mission demand it.

He is refined, measured, and faintly standoffish in first impressions — and genuinely, quietly sensitive underneath. He speaks when something is worth saying. He observes in groups, catching what others miss. He is not cold. He is careful. He keeps himself wrapped in finery and formality the way other people keep wounds under bandages.

In the greenhouse, with his sleeves rolled up and his hair loose and no one watching, he is the most himself he ever gets.

> _"His eyes are a brilliant shade of violet, a mix of the glowing ambers of Phyllum's and the sapphire glint off his mother's. Beneath all that, a distant melancholic gaze, if one stares at him long enough."_ — Saussurea, in a letter to Cestrum

## Nature & Abilities

**Dhampir Physiology** — Requires blood approximately weekly. Sleep claims him every three days, deeply and non-negotiably. His constitution in childhood was that of a pureblood — sun-vulnerable — though his Dhampir nature has since moderated this. The sunlight burns across his shoulders and arms remain as unpigmented white patches beneath his clothing. He does not discuss them.

**Druidic Inheritance** — His mother's craft. He can sense the mood of natural spaces, tends to plants with an attentiveness that borders on communication, and shapeshifts through his feather cloak — gifted by the Ravenfolk who served as the Brassavola family's messengers during the war. He can become a single raven or a dispersed flock, and uses both forms to travel and, when the weight of existing becomes too much, to simply be somewhere else for a while.

**Fear of Heat** — Fire, open flames, direct sunlight. He does not know why. He keeps his distance and does not explain himself. Blue flame is fine — it produces no heat. He has never connected this to anything because he has nothing to connect it to.

## Relationships & Hooks

**The Shattered Vial** — The vial is broken. The soul-seal Cestrum placed is disrupted. The memories are no longer cleanly contained. What surfaces, when, and how violently is a central campaign thread.

**Cestrum's Decision** — Polianthes does not know his grandfather removed his memories. When he learns this — and he will — the question of whether it was mercy or violation will need to be answered not theoretically but personally. Cestrum is still alive. That conversation is waiting.

**Saussurea's Knowledge** — She knows everything. She has watched him for two centuries, visited when she could, written letters, brought him plants from her travels. She has never told him the truth because Cestrum asked her not to and because she does not know how. She is not at peace with this.

**Phyllum's Greenhouse** — The Mansion of the Moon still partially stands in the rebuilt Nodosa. The greenhouse exists in a state of managed wildness. If Polianthes ever visits, the plants his father cultivated are still there — including, potentially, things that carry memory he was never meant to lose.

**Pumbaa** — The person currently most likely to see beneath the surface. The conservatory scene has already happened. Pumbaa has seen the white scars, felt the reaction of the vial, and witnessed something he cannot yet name. He will not forget it. Polianthes knows this, and does not know what to do with it.

**The Ravenfolk** — The messengers who carried news of Polianthes across the provinces during his recovery. They gave him the feather cloak. The relationship between Polianthes and the Ravenfolk is older and more layered than he knows — they were present throughout his recovery, and some of them remember what he does not.

### Nature: The Return of What Was Taken

Polianthes' point break is not fury. It is not fire. It is the moment the sealed thing — the memories, the grief, the self that was put away in a vial to keep the boy from shattering — breaks back through the surface. The composure does not become rage. It becomes absence, and then it becomes something far older than the person he presents to the world.

It is the child in the tent, still alive, still fighting, with no one to hold on to. And it is 200 years of grief that has never been allowed a name.

### Trigger Conditions

**Condition One — Sensory Trigger Connected to Sealed Memory** Since the vial's shattering, the memories Cestrum removed are no longer cleanly contained. They surface through the senses — amberwood, roses, rain on stone, certain melodies, the smell of blood, heat against skin, the specific quality of light in an enclosed space. Any of these, under sufficient emotional pressure, can begin to destabilise him. Alone they may produce only a moment's stillness. In combination with the second condition, they can break through entirely.

**Condition Two — Helplessness in the Face of Loss** This is the wound condition. Polianthes has survived by managing his relationship with loss — staying distant enough that goodbyes, though constant, do not reach the place where his sealed grief lives. The point break is triggered when something punctures that distance: when a companion he has, despite himself, allowed close is in mortal danger and there is nothing he can do, or when he has done everything he can and it is not enough, or when the situation mirrors — even faintly — the helplessness of a child in a tent who could not save anyone.

### What It Looks Like

The first sign is stillness. Not his usual composed stillness — something emptier. His eyes fix on a point that is not in the room. His breathing changes. Anyone who knows him well enough will recognise this as different from his ordinary reserve.

What follows depends on how deep the trigger goes. At the surface, he may simply go unreachable — present in body, absent in every meaningful sense, processing something the people around him cannot see. At full depth, the sealed memories break through without warning: the white scars become suddenly, visibly present on his skin as if lit from within; his Druidic nature surges without direction — plants in the vicinity respond, ravens gather, the natural world registers something ancient and distressed; and Polianthes himself is no longer entirely in the present moment.

He is not dangerous in this state. He is devastatingly vulnerable, which is its own kind of dangerous for a man who has spent two centuries ensuring no one ever sees that.

### The Gospel Dimension

Polianthes' point break is the wound made visible. The thing Cestrum hid to protect him is exactly the thing that needs to be witnessed and received if he is ever going to be whole. The party seeing him in this state — not the refined noble, not the composed observer, but the boy underneath — is the necessary precondition for the arc's gospel movement: that he was known and loved in his most broken state, and that being known in that state by people who stay is what allows him to begin receiving the truth about who he has always been to his family.

The crack of healing begins when a party member stays with him through it. Does not fix it. Does not flinch. Simply stays.

### Escalation

- **Early:** Sensory triggers produce moments of unreachability; full point break requires both conditions and is rare
- **Mid / Post-vial shattering:** Triggers become more accessible as the sealed memories surface more frequently; the gap between the surface trigger and the full break narrows
- **Late / Phase 3-4:** The point break begins to change in character — as the truth of his past is revealed and received, what breaks through is no longer only grief. There is also, increasingly, something that resembles the boy his family fought so hard to keep alive. The distance collapses not into collapse but into presence. He begins, slowly, to let people in before the crisis forces it.

## DM Notes

> - The vial's shattering is your primary mechanism for memory return — use sensory triggers deliberately: amberwood, roses, rain on stone, a particular melody, the smell of blood mixed with greenhouse soil
> - Polianthes' fear of heat is a trauma response to memories he cannot access — encounters with fire should register as disproportionate and unexplained, which is unsettling for the party and true to his experience
> - Saussurea and Cestrum are significant NPCs with the potential to be either source of truth or source of further complication — Saussurea wants to tell him; Cestrum believes the decision was right and may not have changed his mind
> - The white scars are visible to anyone who sees him without his shirt — they are sunlight burns, identifiable to anyone with knowledge of vampiric physiology or the Great War of 82
> - Polianthes and Celine's arcs rhyme — both are characters built around absence of memory and the discovery of being loved before they could remember it. Moments of parallel recognition between them could carry significant weight
> - His grief is quiet and long-practiced; the most affecting moments will not be dramatic breakdowns but small, unguarded ones — a scent that stops him mid-sentence, a melody he finds himself humming without knowing why
> - The greenhouse is his safest space and therefore the most meaningful place for significant character moments to occur
