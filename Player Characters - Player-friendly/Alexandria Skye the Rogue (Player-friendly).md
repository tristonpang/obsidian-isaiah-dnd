## Overview

Alexandria Skye is a rogue who knows exactly what the right thing to do is in almost any situation. She can identify it quickly, articulate it clearly, and act on it convincingly. She is perceptive, capable, and quietly attentive in a way that makes her both an effective party member and someone people instinctively trust.

She is also, privately, aware that knowing the right thing and fully meaning it are not always the same thing — and that the distance between them is something she has been carrying for longer than she has had words for it.

> _"I know what I should want. I'm working on wanting it."_

---

## Backstory

### The Good Girl

Alexandria grew up in an environment that valued correctness — moral, social, and otherwise. She was quick and attentive, and she learned early that knowing the right answer was noticed and rewarded. She excelled within this framework. She was kind when kindness was called for, honest when honesty was expected, and consistently, reliably good in the ways that were legible to the people around her.

What she noticed, with growing quietness as she got older, was that she could do all of this with very little of herself actually present in it. The actions were right. The words were right. But underneath them was something she could not name — a gap between what she performed and what she felt, between the person she appeared to be and the person she actually was when no one was watching.

She has not discussed this with most people. She is not sure she has the language for it yet.

### The Rogue

The choice of roguery is not incidental. A rogue works in the space between what is seen and what is true — in shadows, in the gap between appearance and reality. Alexandria did not choose this class because she is dishonest. She chose it, perhaps, because it is the first place she has found where the gap is the skill and not the problem. She is good at it. Her intelligence, her ability to read a room, her instinct for what a situation needs — all of it translates.

She is still working out whether the gap she has always lived in is a gift or something she needs to bring into the light. Possibly both.

### The Party

Alexandria joined the party through means that made practical sense, and she has stayed because the work is real and the people are stranger and more genuine than most she has encountered. She watches them with the specific attention of someone who suspects that what they have — some quality of being actually, fully present in their own lives — is something she wants and does not yet know how to find.

She does not say this. But she notices.

## Character

Alexandria presents as composed, warm, and socially precise. She gives people what they need and is well-liked for it. Her most unguarded moments arrive accidentally — a flash of real laughter, a response that came out before she could shape it, a moment of frustration that is more honest than she intended. These moments flicker and are quickly recovered. But they are the truest things about her.

She is capable of genuine goodness. She has experienced it, in glimpses — moments where what she did and what she meant were the same thing, where the action came from somewhere real rather than somewhere managed. She wants more of those moments. She does not entirely know how to make them happen.

What she is learning, slowly, is that they cannot be manufactured. They can only be received.

---

## Motivation

Alexandria is not sure she has a clear motivation for adventuring beyond the practical — she is here, she is useful, and the alternative was less interesting. But underneath the practical, something else is present: a longing for the kind of life where who she is and what she does finally occupy the same space. Where she is not managing the distance between them but has simply closed it.

She does not have a roadmap for this. She suspects the road is going to find her rather than the other way around.

---

## What People Notice About Alexandria

She is perceptive — she misses very little. She is warm — people feel attended to around her. She is reliable — she does what she says, goes where she is needed, holds the line when it matters. She is also, to those paying close enough attention, occasionally slightly ahead of herself — giving the right response a half-second before she has fully inhabited it.

The moments where she is fully present — not managing, not performing, just there — are the best version of her. They are becoming more frequent. She does not always notice when they happen. The people around her do.
